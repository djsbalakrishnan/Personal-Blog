### 🔧 LeetCode Problem of the Week

This week, we are diving into a classic linked list problem. Here is the question:

> Given the beginning of a linked list head, return true if there is a cycle in the linked list. Otherwise, return false.

You can find the problem right here: [Cycle Detection](https://neetcode.io/problems/linked-list-cycle-detection/question)

A straightforward solution is to use a hash set and track visited nodes. If a node appears again, a cycle exists. This gives `O(n)` time and `O(n)` space. But what if you want to reduce the space complexity?

That is where Floyd’s Cycle-Finding Algorithm comes in. It is one of the most elegant patterns in linked list problems. Give it a try. 


### 🌍 Projects & Articles I’m Excited About
Building *Ninaivu* ; A Spaced Repetition Platform for Tech Interview Prep

I've been teaching Data Structures & Algorithms and Data Engineering for a while now, and one thing I keep noticing is that students cram, pass, and forget. Not because they aren't smart, but because the way most people study is fundamentally broken.

Re-reading notes doesn't work. Passive review doesn't work. What the science consistently shows is that two things do: [spaced repetition](https://youtu.be/Z-zNHHpXoMM?si=l_InWKfklddtC2QT) (reviewing material at increasing intervals, right before you forget it) and [active recall](https://youtu.be/fDbxPVn02VU?si=m3X12ySfQoYw983a) (forcing your brain to retrieve information rather than just recognise it).

*Ninaivu* is a platform where students can log in, pick from pre-built decks across DSA topics (Arrays, Trees, Dynamic Programming, Graphs) and Data Engineering frameworks (Spark, Kafka, AWS Glue, Airflow), or create their own cards. The system tracks when they last revised each topic, scores their readiness, and tells them exactly where the gaps are before an interview.

More updates soon. If you're a student preparing for tech interviews and want early access, reach out.
